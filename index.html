Class: March 1

Today we met our groups and talked about "Chocolate Covered Broccoli". I think that this is a really easy trap to fall into when creating an educational game as, when the goal is to educate moreso than create a fun game, it can tend to rely on weak mechanics to make the learning fun. The easiest way to avoid this is by making a game in which the gameplay is tied to the learning but not dependent on it. This helps to avoid overly simple games and to make the game fun before focusing too much on the learning aspect.

We later discussed whether or not certain games were chocolate covered broccoli. Our game was Typing of the Dead which is more or less a typing simulator with zombies in it. We collectively decided that this game was chocolate covered broccoli because the core mechanic was typing. That said it looked like a fantastic game and I would play it.

Today we talked about how to affect change on the players of our games. Despite what it may sound like, I actually consider myself to have great opinions on all things and to be fairly left leaning. I do not fully agree with the methods to affect change proposed in class today. There is a line close to the beginning of the powerpoint about educating kids on how they can affect positive change. I do not think that educating kids how they can affect change is a way that they can actually affect change. I fear that it runs the risk of turning that kid into an annoying little propaganda bot for whatever it is you are telling them but more likely it just makes the kid bored. I think that for certain issues, a more effective approach is simply educating kids on the issues and allowing them to decide whether or not to care about it on their own is the most effective way to affect change. Realistically, these kids are not gonna make any type of good change until they are all grown up and taking over the world. I think this has been proven to be true by children taking the stage such as Greta Thunberg. I agree completely with what she is saying, but sadly, because she is a child, she is simply incapable of reaching those that disagree with her because an adult that doesn't believe in climate change isn't gonna be like maybe I am wrong because this kid disagrees with me. These kids are the future and they will be able to affect enormous change when their time comes.

This in mind, I think that the most effective way to affect this change is to instill importance in specific issues that kids like and then show how the world is going to affect these issues. This will make the kid go home and internalize these issues so that they can potentially take up an interest in them that will stick with them their whole life. Then you have an educated adult down the line rather than a kid that simply gets told to stop spouting off whatever the recent issue their school made them learn about. I have republican parents so I should know. As a kid coming home and being like "today at school I learned about climate change" and watching their reactions did have an affect on me, and I adopted their policies over my school's because I trust my parents way more than my teachers (no offense professor judging from the slide's desire to create change I probably agree with you much more than them now). I think this is the case often and so, in an effort to avoid providing nothing but hollow phrases to be shot down at home, I think we should try to instill a true interest in a subject and let that blossom into advocism down the line.

Homework 2: What makes up a game?

For this assignment we were tasked with analyzing and suggesting improvements for a simple pen and paper game and comparing two dissimilar games. For my games I picked a rail tycoon boardgame called Ticket to Ride alongside Call of Duty Warzone. I thought these would be fun to compare because they are wildly different in gameplay but they are rather similar in the progression of the game. I made the statement that both games are driven by positioning and economy moreso than skill. Warzone is still highly skill dependent but a worse team can still beat a better team with better consumables (bought with money), tactics, and position. In Ticket to Ride a good established main rail in a highly populated area can spell a late game snowball where the player no longer has to place roads and can just acquire already route cards which they have almost completed already given their position on the board.

For the game analysis section I wasn't exactly sure on the rules of the game so I proposed multiple rulechanges for each possible gamerules. That game could only be so fun though so there was not a huge room for improvement.
